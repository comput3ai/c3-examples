Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace, commonly known as Ada Lovelace, was born on December 10, 1815, in London, England, to Lord George Gordon Byron and his wife, Anne Isabella Milbanke. Her father, the famous poet Lord Byron, left her mother shortly after Ada's birth, and she had little contact with him throughout her life. This event would later influence Ada's relationship with mathematics and science, as her mother encouraged her to pursue these subjects to counterbalance any potential "poetic" tendencies inherited from her father.

**Early Life and Education**

Ada's early education was focused on mathematics and science, which were considered unconventional subjects for women at the time. Her mother hired tutors to teach Ada arithmetic, algebra, and geometry, as well as languages such as French and Italian. In 1833, Ada met Mary Somerville, a Scottish mathematician and scientist who became her mentor and friend. Somerville introduced Ada to Charles Babbage, an English mathematician and inventor, who was working on his proposed mechanical general-purpose computer, the Analytical Engine.

**Relationship with Charles Babbage**

Ada's relationship with Charles Babbage began in 1833, when she attended a lecture he gave on his Analytical Engine. She was fascinated by the machine's potential to go beyond mere calculation and perform any task that could be expressed in a series of steps. Over the next several years, Ada corresponded with Babbage, discussing mathematics, science, and philosophy. In 1842-1843, she translated an article on the Analytical Engine by Italian mathematician Luigi Menabrea, adding her own notes and comments, which ended up being longer than the original article.

**Contributions to Early Computing**

Ada's work on the Analytical Engine, as expressed in her notes, is considered a groundbreaking contribution to computer science. She recognized the machine's potential to go beyond mere calculation and perform any task that could be expressed in a series of steps. Her notes include what is considered to be the first computer program, a method for calculating Bernoulli numbers using the Analytical Engine. This work demonstrates her understanding of the machine's capabilities and her ability to think abstractly about its potential applications.

**Lasting Legacy**

Ada Lovelace's legacy extends far beyond her work on the Analytical Engine. She is now recognized as a pioneer in computer science, and her contributions have inspired generations of mathematicians, scientists, and programmers. In 1979, the programming language Ada was named after her, and she has been celebrated in numerous books, films, and exhibitions.
